The Tax

(10 min. read)

I am part of a pox that is actively making the world worse every day. You may have met someone like me before, maybe at a party. You approach this man, he seems nice enough. You strike up conversation, interested in learning more about him. He responds by describing the difference between actual and sanctifying Grace. Huh. You’re not really interested in this so you try to pivot to another topic. But your pivot fails and now he’s describing what a purificator is and that you can’t use it as a corporal cloth at Mass. It’s some real riveting stuff. Your eyes slide over to the door, maybe you can make a break for it? But it’d be rude to just leave. What is he talking about now? Hyperdulia? Latria? Oh, wait. He needs to grab his Missal to show you an example. Now’s your chance. You scramble through the exit into the cold night air.

Outside there’s someone else. He’s crouching over a pile of logs. You thought you’d gotten away, but you just walked into the snare of another one. He’s now explaining proper fire making techniques. Oh brother. It’s just firemaking. It’s not some masculine black magic. Don’t tell me he’s gonna start talking about grilling next. Is that someone else walking up? You blurt out in a panic “Oh hey how are you?” The new person pauses. He blinks, and launches into describing the Scott Hahn book he was reading today.

Overconfidant young Catholic men. Our purpose in life is to tell you facts, dispense unasked-for wisdom, or tell you about some rule the church has. Ultimately our problem is how agonizingly practical we are. There’s always something concrete and practical for us to talk about. We rarely have time for the beautiful and sublime. Next time you meet someone like us, just wait. Wait and see how long it takes him to solve a problem or tell you a fact.
“You know, technically you’re not supposed to…”
“You know the church used to do it this way…”
“Priest’s don’t follow this rule, but according to the Missal they need to…”
I heard the Catholic male population has a rate of engineers almost twice the total male population. That doesn’t surprise me at all.

If you’re detecting bitterness in my voice, know that it’s genuine. Also you should know that of all these offenders I am the first and greatest. So if I'm going to write about faith on this blog I need to limit myself. I don’t want to contribute to the hoards talking about rules, solutions, and facts. I want to be on the side extolling truth and beauty. I’m not Catholic because I heard the most interesting fact or liked some rule. I’m Catholic because I found someone I’m madly in love with; I fell in love with Christ.

So here’s a rule for this blog, inspired by legendary RPG blogger Joesky: https://joeskythedungeonbrawler.wordpress.com/2010/07/12/house-rule-for-osr-and-not-osr-games-people-blogs/ Every spiritual post must be about something beautiful. If I have finished a post and all I've done is offer advice or give facts, I’ll end meditating on a poem.

And guess what? This post is about a rule so I’m starting immediately. Post is over. Now for the poem that’s actually pretty topical.

Spring and Fall

by Gerard Many Hopkins

Hopkins can be hard to read, partially because he was working with a form of English with straight up different definitions for words than we do. So here’s a summary of the poem: The poet addresses a child (Margaret) who is stopped in her tracks by how a tree changes color in fall. The poet says she won’t always react to the tree like this. When she’s older she’ll walk past these trees and not even spare a thought for them. The poem’s highest point is this line where she “will weep and know why.” She’ll have this cold heart, and some part of her will know what she’s lost. “It is Margaret you will mourn for.”
If you’ve never read Hopkins I recommend reading the poem aloud. For me the opening lines are kinda awkward compared to his other work, but the rest of the poem’s flow is unmatched.

Márgarét, áre you gríeving
Over Goldengrove unleaving?
Leáves like the things of man, you
With your fresh thoughts care for, can you?
Ah! ás the heart grows older
It will come to such sights colder
By and by, nor spare a sigh
Though worlds of wanwood leafmeal lie;
And yet you wíll weep and know why.
Now no matter, child, the name:
Sórrow’s spríngs áre the same.
Nor mouth had, no nor mind, expressed
What heart heard of, ghost guessed:
It ís the blight man was born for,
It is Margaret you mourn for.

(image)
Anyone with children in their life has experienced a child’s wonder at benign things.There’s a whole dimension to the world they see clearly with their “fresh thoughts”. This adds to Christ’s teaching of “turn and become like children” (MT 18:3 (link)). Childlike faith is typically interpreted as trusting. Kids don’t worry about who is picking them up, they just assume when a stranger picks them up they are going somewhere good. But I don’t think that’s all of it. There is a veil that children are able to see through. We look at the same things, but a child is transfixed by it while we walk past without a second thought.

An adult could walk through a “world” of leaves on the ground (wanwood leafmeal) without any emotion. And we know what’s happened to us we will “weep and know why.” Ultimately the poem ends on a sad and resigned note.

(nature picture)
It ís the blight man was born for,
It is Margaret you mourn for.

Is it possible to recapture part of this wonder and awe? On some level. But it’ll never come as easy as it was. And I believe it’ll never be on the same level as children. The poem doesn’t mention this, but I wonder if there’s a small part of this that actually grows stronger with age? I look out at the retired folk walking through the park in the morning. I see men with painful backs still going out to fish and look out over the water. I see women spending more and more time watching birds. I haven't reached that point yet, God willing I'll live long enough to see it. Maybe when I retire I'll start to see through that veil that eluded me through all of my adulthood. Or maybe when I reach that age I'll see just how important seeing through the veil was, and work so much harder to recapture it.
